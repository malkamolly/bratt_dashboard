@welcome
eyebrow: How Galls Form
title: A tiny insect convinces the tree to build it a house.
subtitle: Wild biology — but harmless to the tree in almost every case.
body: **Galls form during the accelerated growth period of new leaves, shoots, and flowers** in late spring. Cynipid wasps damage the plant by feeding, and their **salivary secretions cause the tree to over-produce growth hormones.**
body: Higher hormone production means **increased cell size or cell numbers in localized spots** — that's the gall. The insect feeds and grows inside it.
body: **Mature plant tissues aren't affected.** And once galls start forming, they keep going even after the insect dies. Gall makers basically reprogram meristematic tissue.


@three-rules
eyebrow: What To Tell The Client
title: The honest answer is "do nothing."
subtitle: Three rules for the conversation.
rule: Not Harmful | Galls do not harm the tree. Very young trees covered with galls for several years in a row could be weakened — but that's the exception, not the rule.
rule: No Treatment Needed | We do not spray for gall-making insects. If a homeowner insists on doing something for cosmetic reasons, **soil-injected imidacloprid** is labeled for it.
rule: Older Galls Don't Drop | Galls are now part of the twig. Even after treatment they don't fall off — they shed slowly over time. Set that expectation.

@image-side
eyebrow: 1 of 3 · Red Oak Apple Gall
title: The yellow-green one in your hand.
subtitle: "Creepy oak galls that are fun to dissect and look delicious."
body: **Found on red oak family** — the bristle-tipped, two-year-acorn oaks. The gall is the size of a small apple, **greenish-yellow with brown speckles.**
body: Inside is a small chamber where the larva develops. **Fun to dissect** — they look strangely like fruit but are 100% inedible.
body: **Cosmetic only.** Drops off the tree late summer or fall after the larva matures.

fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: 1 of 3 · Red Oak Apple Gall
title: Inside the apple gall.
subtitle: A single chamber. A single larva. That's the whole story.
body: The cross-section shows what's actually going on — a hollow gall with **a small mass at the center where the larva grows.** The plant material around it is just an engineered nursery.
body: **Once the larva emerges**, the gall dries out and falls. There's nothing left to treat by the time the homeowner notices it.

@image-side
eyebrow: 2 of 3 · Jumping Oak Gall
title: Neuroterus saltatorius
subtitle: Looks like confetti on the leaf.
body: **Caused by cynipidae** — small stingless wasps. **Non-lethal. Cosmetic only.**
body: Called "jumping" because the **wasp larva is moving around inside the gall.** They often fall off the leaf as they near maturity — sometimes you'll find them hopping on the ground under an infested tree.
body: **Found on white oak and bur oak** — the rounded-lobe, one-year-acorn oaks.
image: /topics/oak-galls/jumping-gall.jpg
fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: 3 of 3 · Oak Bullet Gall
title: Disholcaspis quercusmamma
subtitle: Round, bullet-shaped galls clustered on twigs.
body: **Caused by cynipid wasps.** Adult lays an egg on the leaf or twig and the tree grows a shell around it. The wasp bores its way out when ready to fly.
body: **July and August: thousands of tiny stingless wasps emerge.** They're not interested in you. The galls don't disrupt vascular function inside the twig — the tree's fine.
body: **Found on bur oak**, less commonly on swamp white (bicolor) oak.
image: /topics/oak-galls/bullet-gall.jpeg
fit: cover


@welcome
eyebrow: The Wasp Nest Mistake
title: Yellow jackets feeding on bullet galls aren't nesting.
subtitle: The single most common client question on this topic.
body: Bullet galls **secrete nectar on the outside.** That nectar attracts **yellow jackets and other wasps** — but those wasps aren't nesting in the galls.
body: **They're just feeding.** The galls themselves are still cynipid-wasp galls. No nest, no swarm, no aggression — just hungry yellow jackets at a sugar source.
body: **Tell the client this directly.** It's the difference between "we need pest control" and "this is normal and we leave it alone."


@three-rules
eyebrow: The Sales Talk
title: How to close a "what about these weird things?" call.
subtitle: Most of these calls don't need a visit at all.
rule: Lead With "It's Fine" | "Those are galls — they're cosmetic. The tree isn't being harmed. We'd recommend doing nothing." That sentence saves a truck roll on most of these.
rule: Offer The Option | If the client really wants something done — for aesthetics, for peace of mind — soil-injected imidacloprid is the tool. Set expectations: it works for next year, not this one.
rule: Set The Yellow-Jacket Expectation | If they're bullet galls, mention the wasp activity proactively. "You'll see yellow jackets feeding on them — they're not nesting, just snacking. Leave them alone."
