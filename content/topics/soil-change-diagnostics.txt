@welcome
eyebrow: Why This Matters
title: Half of our "mystery decline" calls are this.
subtitle: When a tree starts dying for "no reason" — it's almost never no reason.
body: When a client says "my tree was fine last year and now it's dying," **the first instinct is to look for disease or pests.** That's backwards.
body: Most mystery declines trace to a **change in the soil environment** — construction, a new retaining wall, an irrigation tweak, a regrade. Often the homeowner didn't connect those events to the tree.
body: Our job: **scan the property for evidence of recent change** before we start chasing biotic causes. The right diagnosis is often the one nobody thought to ask about.


@card-grid
eyebrow: The Four Mechanisms
title: How human activity kills a tree's root system.
subtitle: All four can be subtle. All four are common.
card: Soil Compaction | Heavy equipment, fill, or foot traffic squeezes the porosity out of the soil. Fibrous roots can't survive.
card: Root Damage | Trenching, foundation digs, retaining walls, utility runs — cutting roots cuts the tree's lifelines.
card: Grade Change | Raising or lowering the soil level around the trunk. Absorbing roots get buried or removed.
card: Water Redirection | Construction next door changes how water flows. Trees drown, or dry out, depending which way it tips.


@section-divider
number: 01
title: Soil Compaction
tagline: The first step of any construction project is to strip off the soil that makes trees thrive.


@welcome
eyebrow: Mechanism 1
title: Compacted soil holds no air and no water.
subtitle: Common on new construction, commercial sites, newer suburbs.
body: **Topsoil is porous, therefore unsuitable for building** — so construction crews strip it off first. What's left is compacted clay or fill. Fibrous roots cannot survive in it.
body: **Dozers, trucks, excavators, and piles of fill all cause compaction.** Once the soil is compressed, water runs across the surface rather than penetrating to the root zone.
body: **Don't be fooled by lush turf on top.** Sod over compacted soil looks fine for years while the tree above it slowly suffocates.
body: **Fun fact:** the same thing happens in cow pastures. You'll see stone-dead trees in fields with cows grazing under them. The hooves compress the soil — small surface area, lots of weight — and the tree's root zone never recovers.


@section-divider
number: 02
title: Root Damage
tagline: Trenching, retaining walls, utility runs. Mechanical cuts to the root system.


@welcome
eyebrow: Mechanism 2
title: Below-grade damage hides in plain sight.
subtitle: The damage happened months ago. The decline shows up now.
body: **Mostly seen after digging a foundation, cutting in a retaining wall or fence, or trenching utilities** — underground power, sewer, water, irrigation, drain tile.
body: **Scan the property for evidence of these events.** We over-diagnose disease before we ever evaluate for mechanical damage underground. That's the first move.
body: **Distance matters.** Trees can sometimes recover if the cut was far from the trunk. The closer to the root flare, the worse the damage — and the lower the chance of survival.
body: **A recent example:** the city trenched fiber through the boulevard in front of my house about 8 weeks ago, and half the easement trees are dead already. The trench started right next to the trunks.
body: **An old example:** Stan Bratt's neighbor, ten years ago. 30-inch Bur Oak. They cut right up to the root flare on one side to dig a foundation for a home addition. It slowly declined and eventually tipped over onto the house.


@section-divider
number: 03
title: Grade Change
tagline: Raising or lowering the dirt level around the tree. Both kill, slowly.


@welcome
eyebrow: Mechanism 3
title: Most absorbing roots are in the top 8–12 inches.
subtitle: Bury them or remove them and the tree starves.
body: **Raising the grade** acts like compaction — covering the roots with fill means it takes much more water to reach absorbing depth. The roots starve for both water and air.
body: **Lowering the grade** removes the absorbing roots outright. Less common, because a regrade usually involves removing the tree first — but we see it on partial regrades.
body: **The diagnostic tell:** an older tree with **no visible root flare** at ground level. If the trunk goes straight into the soil like a fence post, the grade was raised on it at some point. Recently-raised: tree still looks healthy. Old grade change: tree is probably already dead from girdling roots.
body: **A war story:** a customer called once to ask if their trees would be okay after the contractor raised the grade "a little bit" and then lowered it back to normal a week or two later. The contractor sent photos of mature oaks buried under **maybe 10 feet of fill.** They asked what they could do to save them. We told them to call their lawyer. The trees were dug back out with an excavator and died shortly after.


@section-divider
number: 04
title: Water Redirection
tagline: Construction changes the way water moves. The tree gets flooded — or stranded.


@welcome
eyebrow: Mechanism 4
title: It doesn't have to be on the client's property.
subtitle: A neighbor's project can be the cause.
body: **Construction projects — even in a neighbor's yard — can change the direction or volume of water flowing into the homeowner's yard.** A regraded driveway upstream becomes a new drainage problem downstream.
body: **In extreme cases**, water pools above the root system and **literally drowns the roots.** Fungal problems get worse with excessive moisture at the base of the tree: Armillaria root rot, canker diseases.
body: **Saturated soil also destabilizes trees.** There can be so much water that there's not enough solid ground for the roots to grip — trees can simply fall over, especially in a wind event.
body: **Spruces and white pines** are especially vulnerable. They capture so much wind that a slight loss of root grip is enough to topple them. We get calls every year about an uprooted conifer after a heavy rain.


@three-rules
eyebrow: The Diagnostic Move
title: Three things to ask before chasing disease.
subtitle: Run through these on every "mystery decline" call.
rule: What's Changed Recently? | New construction, retaining wall, fence, irrigation work, paving, regrade — anywhere on this property or the neighbors'. Get specifics.
rule: Walk The Property | Look for the physical evidence — trenches refilled with different soil, footings near the trunk, missing root flare, water pooling near the base, fresh sod.
rule: Then Look At The Tree | After ruling in or out an abiotic cause, then evaluate for disease and pests. This order saves you from misdiagnosing 30% of decline calls.
