@section-divider
number: 01
title: Where Apple Trees Come From
tagline: A 2-minute origin story for context.


@image-side
eyebrow: Apple Tree Origins
title: 400 years of human intervention.
subtitle: A few origin facts that come up in client conversations.
body: **Discovered in central Asia, introduced to North America by the French in the 1600s.** Apples as we know them are the result of thousands of years of selective breeding.
body: **Apple seeds don't grow true to seed** — plant a seed from a Honeycrisp and you'll get something else, probably a crabapple. **Commercial apple trees are clones** (grafted onto crabapple rootstock).
body: **Apples generally need a separate pollinator tree.** Innumerable exceptions, but if a homeowner asks why their lone apple tree barely produces — pollination is the most likely answer.

title: Why We Prune
tagline: Three reasons that justify the cuts.


@three-rules
eyebrow: Why Prune Apple Trees?
title: The three actual reasons.
subtitle: All three matter for fruit, structure, and the homeowner's quality of life.
rule: Fruit Production | Apples form on **spur buds from last year's growth**, not on this year's new shoots. Pruning balances "keep the fruit buds" against "don't let the tree get tangled."
rule: Tree Structure | Compact branches with light reaching all of them produces better fruit and a healthier tree. **Strong central leader, no crossing branches, no crowding.**
rule: Manageability | A residential apple tree the homeowner can pick from — and that doesn't break under fruit weight — beats a wild 30-foot specimen every time.


@image-side
eyebrow: Fruit Buds
title: Spur buds produce apples. Don't cut them off.
subtitle: The single most important thing to know before you prune.
body: **Fruit forms on "spur" buds — stubby, knobby short shoots that come off older wood.** Last year's spur buds are likely to produce fruit this year. Cut those off and you've cut off the harvest.
body: **New growth (this year's long shoots) won't fruit this year.** Buds beyond the previous year's reduction point are unlikely to produce fruit.
body: **Reduction cuts** are how we shorten branches without removing fruiting wood. Aim for cuts that preserve the spur buds along the older wood.
image: /topics/pruning-apple-trees/fruit-buds.jpeg
fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: Ideal Structure
title: What a well-pruned apple tree looks like.
subtitle: Older growth preserved, newer growth shortened, light everywhere.
body: **Older growth is preserved** (that's where the fruiting spurs live). **Newer growth is shortened** (so the tree doesn't get top-heavy or tangled).
body: **Compact branches that can physically withstand the weight of fruit.** Apple branches break under load — structure matters more than for an ornamental tree.
body: **Branches all exposed to light.** **Strong central leader.** Minimal crowding. Upright water-sprout growth is controlled, not allowed to take over the canopy.

@three-rules
eyebrow: Talking To Clients
title: When a homeowner asks about their apple tree.
subtitle: Three answers that cover most of the calls.
rule: "Will You Prune It For Fruit?" | "We'll prune it for structure and health, which helps with fruit. We're not orchardists — we won't promise more apples, but the tree will be in better shape to produce."
rule: "Why Doesn't It Produce Apples?" | Most likely: no pollinator nearby, the tree got pruned too hard (cut off the spur buds), or last year's spring weather killed the blossoms. Walk through each before recommending anything.
rule: "When Should You Prune?" | **Late winter / early spring** before bud break. Avoid pruning in summer (stresses the tree) or fall (creates wounds that don't close before winter).
