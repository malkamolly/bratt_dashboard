@three-rules
eyebrow: The Headline
title: Three things showing up on lilacs.
subtitle: Two foliar diseases that look identical, and a separate chlorosis issue.
rule: Pseudomonas | Bacterial blight. **No proven treatment.** Manage by pruning out and bolstering plant health. The bad-news call.
rule: Pseudocercospora | Fungal leaf spot. **Treatable** — two fungicide sprays in spring. The good-news call.
rule: Iron Chlorosis | Different issue entirely — nutrient deficiency, not a pathogen. Treatable with iron injection. Yellow leaves, green veins.


@section-divider
number: 01
title: The Two Look-Alikes
tagline: Pseudomonas and Pseudocercospora present similarly. Treatment is wildly different.


@image-side
eyebrow: Pseudomonas syringae
title: Bacterial Blight.
subtitle: Wilting shoots, blackened stems, dark brown leaf spots.
body: **Bacterial disease.** Presents with **wilting shoots and blackened stems/flower clusters** — visually similar to diplodia on pines or apple scab on leaves.
body: **Bacteria are basically everywhere.** Spreads through open wounds. Can be **pruned out** the same way we manage fireblight — sanitization cuts, sterile tools between cuts.
body: **There is currently no proven treatment for this disease.** As with all diseases without a direct treatment, **bolstering plant health and improving soil conditions** is what extends plant life. That's the conversation.
image: /topics/lilac-foliar-diseases/pseudomonas.png
fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: Pseudocercospora
title: Lilac Leaf Spot.
subtitle: Fungal disease that looks just like the bacterial one.
body: **Fungal disease.** **Commonly confused with pseudomonas** — same shoot wilt, similar leaf-spot appearance. But this one is treatable, so the differential matters.
body: **Treated like apple scab** (and most other deciduous foliar fungi): **two sprays in the spring with fungicide** as leaves emerge.
body: When in doubt between this and pseudomonas, treat for the fungus first — at worst you've done no harm; at best you've solved the problem.
image: /topics/lilac-foliar-diseases/pseudocercospora.jpeg
fit: cover


@section-divider
number: 02
title: Iron Chlorosis
tagline: A separate problem on a lot of the same trees. Yellow leaves, green veins.


@image-side
eyebrow: Iron Chlorosis
title: Yellow leaves, green veins.
subtitle: Not a disease — a nutrient absorption problem.
body: **Yellowing leaves caused by iron deficiency.** The diagnostic tell: **yellow leaf with green veins** (the veins stay supplied longest).
body: Lack of iron decreases the plant's ability to produce chlorophyll. **Generally does not kill trees** but makes them look bad and reduces vigor over time.
body: **Manganese deficiency can cause similar chlorosis** in high-pH soils. Even in iron-rich soil, **high pH can lock the iron up as a solid** that roots can't absorb. So this is really a soil-chemistry problem, not just a nutrient deficiency.
image: /topics/lilac-foliar-diseases/iron-chlorosis.png
fit: cover


@welcome
eyebrow: Treatment
title: When and how to treat chlorosis.
subtitle: Different dosing in summer vs fall. Pick the right one.
body: **Summer dosing:** low-dose MNJet FE injection. Treats the current season only — **does not carry over to next year.** Reduced dose is intentional: full dosage in summer can defoliate the tree.
body: **Fall dosing:** **full dose, two seasons of effect.** This is the better-value treatment when timing allows.
body: **Rule of thumb:** if you're talking to a client in spring or summer about chlorosis, set them up for fall treatment unless they need immediate relief. The math favors waiting.


@three-rules
eyebrow: Talking To Clients
title: The conversation, three beats.
subtitle: Each issue has a different talk track. Match yours to the differential.
rule: Pseudomonas | "This is bacterial — there's no spray that fixes it. We can prune out the worst of it and focus on overall plant health, but the lilac will likely keep showing this on and off."
rule: Pseudocercospora | "This looks like the bacterial one but it's actually fungal — which means we *can* treat it. Two spring sprays and your lilac will look much better next year."
rule: Iron Chlorosis | "The yellow leaves with green veins are an iron uptake issue, not a disease. We can inject iron directly into the trunk. Fall treatment is the best value — two seasons of effect."
