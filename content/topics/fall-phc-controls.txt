@welcome
eyebrow: Why Fall Matters
title: The diagnostic window before everything freezes.
subtitle: Three questions worth working through on every fall site visit.
body: **What has changed since summer?** A tree that looked fine in July can show fall symptoms (needle shedding, premature browning, late chlorosis) that point to issues for next year.
body: **Conifer vs deciduous diagnostics differ in fall.** Conifers hold all their tells; deciduous trees lose half their diagnostic surface area to the ground.
body: **What if we can't positively ID the pest right now?** Sometimes the answer is "treat early next spring based on what we saw this summer" — not "diagnose deeper in October."


@section-divider
number: 01
title: Conifer Fall Factors
tagline: They keep their needles — so they keep their diagnostics.


@image-side
eyebrow: Needle Shedding
title: Old needles drop. New needles stay. Don't panic.
subtitle: Common for needles 2–3 years old, pronounced on white pines.
body: **All conifers shed older needles** — typically anything **2–3 years and older.** This is normal, healthy, expected.
body: **Pronounced on white pines** — clients call about it every fall. The diagnostic move: check that **the new and second-year needles are still attached and green.** If only the inner-canopy old needles are shedding, it's normal.
body: **Not harmful. Not treatable. Not necessary to address.** The conversation is reassurance, not diagnosis.
image: /topics/fall-phc-controls/needle-shedding.jpeg
fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: Larch / Tamarack
title: Larix laricina — the deciduous conifer.
subtitle: Don't be that guy who thinks the larch is dying.
body: **Larch (Tamarack)** is a **coniferous tree** in the Pinaceae family — but **not "evergreen."** Needles are soft and short, cones small.
body: **Drops all its needles every fall** in a stunning yellow display, then re-grows them in spring. **This is normal.**
body: If a client calls panicked about their "dying pine" turning yellow in October — there's a 50/50 chance they have a larch. Walk through the ID before you walk through diagnostics.

@section-divider
number: 02
title: Deciduous Fall Factors
tagline: The leaves on the ground tell you what the tree was doing all year.


@image-side
eyebrow: Reading The Canopy In Fall
title: Did treatment work? Compare side by side.
subtitle: We sprayed this tree in May. It's now November.
body: **A treated crabapple in November still has its leaves.** Untreated crabapples nearby will be nearly bare — apple scab defoliated them in late summer.
body: **This side-by-side is one of our best sales artifacts.** Before/after, treated/untreated, with the homeowner's own eyes.
body: When in doubt about whether a fall treatment is working, walk the property next year in October and compare to a neighbor. The answer is usually obvious.

@welcome
eyebrow: The Big Picture
title: A leaf is a snapshot.
subtitle: This year's leaf has this year's problems. We treat for next year.
body: **Fungal diseases** can be sprayed just after bud break, preventing symptoms on mature leaves. **Chlorosis** can be treated at fall dosage now — effective for two seasons.
body: **Poor soil fertility** can be addressed until the ground freezes — or after it thaws in spring. **Spring rains** can change next year's leaves for better or worse on fungal issues.
body: **Insect damage on this year's leaf has zero effect on next year's leaf.** We get new ones every spring. The job is preventing the same damage from happening again.


@three-rules
eyebrow: The Fall Diagnostic Move
title: Three habits that make fall site visits worth doing.
subtitle: Use the season — don't fight it.
rule: Trust Summer's Diagnosis | "My crabapple looked bad in July" — we know what that is. Don't get distracted by November leaf debris. Plan the spring treatment.
rule: Lean On The Ground Layer | Look at the leaves on the ground. Petioles still attached to twigs (oak)? Buds with weird discoloration? The litter tells the story.
rule: Set Up The Watering Conversation | Most homeowners stop watering when the leaves drop. That's exactly when the roots most need water — see the next slide.


@welcome
eyebrow: Fall Watering
title: Water after leaf drop, until the ground freezes.
subtitle: This is the single most important PHC habit we can teach a homeowner.
body: **Root hairs ("absorbing roots") do 90% of the water uptake** for the tree. They mostly live in the top 8–12 inches of soil.
body: **When the top layer dries out for too long, root hairs desiccate and lose function.** A dry fall heading into a dry winter can damage the root system long before the tree shows it in spring.
body: **Watering after leaf drop** — when there's not ample rain — **protects the absorbing roots through winter.** Tell every client this. It costs them nothing but a sprinkler hour and saves trees.
