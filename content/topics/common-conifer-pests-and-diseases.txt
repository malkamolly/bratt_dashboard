@welcome
eyebrow: What These Have In Common
title: One playbook covers most of them.
subtitle: Four fungal diseases and one insect — but the conversation rhymes.
body: **The most common issues we see on conifers.** Easily prevented or eliminated once you can ID them, and lethal if untreated.
body: **Damage is obvious on last year's growth.** That's where you look. Current-year growth typically looks fine until the cycle catches up to it.
body: **Solved by foliar treatment.** All five respond to a spring spray program. Pine Sawfly is the outlier — it's an insect, not a fungus — but timing and conversation are similar.


@three-rules
eyebrow: Before The Detail
title: The non-negotiables.
subtitle: Three things every client needs to hear.
rule: Lethal | Untreated, all five eventually kill the tree. Pine Sawfly the fastest — faster than the fungal diseases. This isn't a cosmetic conversation.
rule: Persistent | The first season won't look dramatically different. Year two starts showing it. Year three is when the tree looks like a tree again.
rule: Timing Matters | We spray as needles or candles emerge. Miss the window and the new growth is unprotected. Fertilize alongside treatment and recovery is faster.

@image-side
eyebrow: 1 of 5 · Rhizosphaera Needle Cast
title: Spruce. Inside-out browning.
subtitle: Most common on **blue (Colorado) spruce.**
body: **Branches defoliate from the center of the tree outward.** Typically identified by **purple needles on the branch interior.**
body: This year's growth appears unaffected. Last year's growth may be purple. Previous years' growth may have already fallen off.
body: **Often paired with spider mite damage** — check for the purple needles, then check for mites. They like the same conditions.
image: /topics/common-conifer-pests-and-diseases/rhizosphaera-1.png
fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: 2 of 5 · Diplodia Shoot Blight
title: Pine. Curled, wilted new shoots.
subtitle: Most common on **red, mugo, and Austrian pines.**
body: New shoots emerge, become infected, **begin to curl** as the needles die back. The curled shoot itself dies.
body: Identified by **brown / yellow / curling shoots on last year's growth.** Easily spotted from a long distance once advanced.
body: Tree can look overall healthy but **covered in patches of dead new growth** — the dead candles are the diagnostic.
image: /topics/common-conifer-pests-and-diseases/diplodia-1.jpeg
fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: 3 of 5 · Dothistroma Needle Blight
title: Pine. Banded needles.
subtitle: Extremely common on **Austrian pine.**
body: **Infects needles, not shoots.** Look for a **red or black band** across the needle, with dead tissue beyond the band.
body: The banding pattern is diagnostic — needles die at the band but the base often stays green for a while.
body: Treated the same as the other fungal blights — three sprays in spring as needles emerge.
image: /topics/common-conifer-pests-and-diseases/dothistroma-1.png
fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: 4 of 5 · Brown Spot Needle Blight
title: Pine. Whole-needle death.
subtitle: Most common on **Scotch and Ponderosa pine** — white pine less so.
body: Appears similar to dothistroma — but the **whole needle dies** rather than pausing at a banded section. Needles can stay green where they attach to the shoot, then drop end of season.
body: **Reddish-brown spots on needles** are the early tell. Scattered through the canopy rather than isolated to one area.
body: **Sanitation helps:** rake up fallen needles, redirect sprinklers off the foliage. Spray three times in the spring like dothistroma.
image: /topics/common-conifer-pests-and-diseases/brownspot-1.jpeg
fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: 5 of 5 · European Pine Sawfly
title: Pine. Larvae feeding in clusters.
subtitle: Most common on **red pine and mugo pine.** Kills trees faster than any of the fungal diseases.
body: Adult **lays eggs inside the needle.** Eggs hatch and **several small larvae feed on individual needles together.** As they grow, each larva consumes a whole needle by itself.
body: **Easy to spot in the lower canopy**, especially on the house side of red and mugo pines. Last year's growth looks chewed up — like rabbit or deer damage. It's not. It's larvae.
body: Infestations can be **identified in winter** as **small white bulges along the needle surface** in a sequential line — that's where eggs were laid.
image: /topics/common-conifer-pests-and-diseases/pine-sawfly.jpeg
fit: cover


@three-rules
eyebrow: How To Talk About These
title: The customer conversation that closes.
subtitle: Three beats, in this order, every time.
rule: Frame The Stakes | "Untreated, this issue will eventually kill the tree. Treating it will get your tree looking healthy and green again — but the tree won't recover by next month."
rule: Set The Timeline | "You'll see improvement next year, with the biggest change by year three. Fertilizing alongside treatment speeds the recovery — looks better faster."
rule: Lead With Value | "Compare the cost of treatment against the cost of replacing a tree this size — and the privacy, the aesthetics, the savings. Treatment usually wins by a wide margin."
