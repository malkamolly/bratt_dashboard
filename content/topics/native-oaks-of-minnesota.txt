@welcome
eyebrow: The Family
title: Fagaceae — Oak, Chestnut, Beech.
subtitle: Quercus, Castanea, Fagus. (Not Aesculus — that's Horse Chestnut.)
body: Characterized by **cupule nuts** where some of the nut is visible inside a cup. Generally **one seed per nut.** High wood density per cubic foot. Slower-growing trees.
body: Oaks **flower as catkins**, not as showy flowers like maples or fruit trees. If you see a hanging tassel in spring, you're probably looking at an oak.
body: Minnesota's native oaks split into two families: **Red Oaks (Erythrobalanus)** and **White Oaks (Leucobalanus).** Knowing which family you're in answers half the ID questions before you look at the leaf.


@two-column
eyebrow: The Big Split
title: Red Oak vs White Oak family.
subtitle: Three differences. Memorize them and you'll never be wrong about the family.
left-header: Red Oaks (Erythrobalanus)
left-icon: ●
left: **Acorns mature in 2 years** — current and last season's acorns both on the tree
left: **Leaf lobes have bristled (pointed) ends**
left: Acorn cup is **smoother**
left: Species: Rubra, Ellipsoidalis, Palustris
right-header: White Oaks (Leucobalanus)
right-icon: ●
right: **Acorns mature in 1 year** — all on this year's growth
right: **Leaf lobes are rounded, no bristles**
right: Acorn cup is **rougher, hairier**
right: Species: Alba, Macrocarpa, Bicolor


@section-divider
number: 01
title: The Red Oak Family
tagline: Bristled lobes. Two-year acorns. Smooth cups.


@image-side
eyebrow: Red Oak
title: Quercus rubra
subtitle: The classic — pointed lobes, glossy leaves, big tree.
body: **The benchmark Red Oak.** Pointed lobes with bristle tips, smooth leaf surface, distinctive striped bark on mature trees.
body: Acorns are medium-sized with a **shallow, smooth cup** covering only the very top of the nut.
image: /topics/native-oaks-of-minnesota/red-oak-leaf.png
fit: contain


@image-side
eyebrow: Red Oak Family
title: Northern Pin Oak — Quercus ellipsoidalis
subtitle: Common in MN. Lobes deeper than red oak, leaves narrower.
body: **Northern Pin Oak** has deeply-cut lobes with sharp, bristled tips. Leaves often appear narrower and more delicate than a true Red Oak.
body: Frequently planted in MN as a landscape replacement for Red Oak. Same family, same diseases (including oak wilt), similar care.
image: /topics/native-oaks-of-minnesota/northern-pin-oak-1.png
fit: contain


@image-side
eyebrow: Red Oak Family
title: Eastern Pin Oak — Quercus palustris
subtitle: Even narrower leaf lobes than Northern Pin. Less common locally.
body: **Eastern Pin Oak** has the **deepest, narrowest lobes** of the Red Oak group. The leaf looks almost lacy compared to a Red or Northern Pin.
body: Native to the eastern US but planted in MN as ornamentals. Watch the lower branches — Pin Oaks often retain a strong **descending lower branch habit** when young, which is a separate ID clue.
image: /topics/native-oaks-of-minnesota/eastern-pin-oak-1.png
fit: contain


@section-divider
number: 02
title: The White Oak Family
tagline: Rounded lobes. One-year acorns. Hairier cups.


@image-side
eyebrow: White Oak
title: Quercus alba
subtitle: The benchmark White Oak — rounded lobes, hairy acorn cups.
body: **Rounded lobes with no bristled tips.** Bark is pale gray, often flaking in long vertical plates on mature trees.
body: Acorns mature in **one season** and the cup is **rough and warty.** A Quercus alba acorn is unmistakable once you've held one.
image: /topics/native-oaks-of-minnesota/white-oak-1.png
fit: contain


@image-side
eyebrow: White Oak Family
title: Bur Oak — Quercus macrocarpa
subtitle: MN's signature White Oak. Massive, fringed acorns.
body: **Bur Oak** has the **largest acorn of any North American oak** — and a distinctive **fringed cup** that covers most of the nut.
body: Bark is deeply ridged and corky. Leaves have a characteristic **fiddle-shaped outline** — narrow at the base, wider toward the tip. Often the easiest oak to ID in MN at a distance.
image: /topics/native-oaks-of-minnesota/bur-oak-1.png
fit: contain


@image-side
eyebrow: White Oak Family
title: Swamp White Oak — Quercus bicolor
subtitle: Two-toned leaves. Tolerates wet sites better than other oaks.
body: **Swamp White Oak** is identified by its **two-toned leaf** — dark green above, pale silvery-green underneath. Wind in the canopy creates a shimmering effect.
body: Tolerates **wetter sites** than most oaks — useful when a client has a low-lying area and wants an oak species that won't sulk.
image: /topics/native-oaks-of-minnesota/swamp-white-oak-1.png
fit: contain


@three-rules
eyebrow: Putting It Together
title: The 30-second ID flow.
subtitle: Three checks, in this order, get you to the answer almost every time.
rule: Look At The Leaf | Pointed, bristled lobes → Red family. Rounded lobes → White family. That's the first 50%.
rule: Look At The Acorn | If there are acorns on the tree from this year AND last year, it's Red family. Only this year's acorns, and a hairy cup → White family.
rule: Then Narrow The Species | Within each family, check leaf depth (Bur is fiddle-shaped, Pin is deeply cut, Swamp White is two-toned). Within minutes, you've got the species.
