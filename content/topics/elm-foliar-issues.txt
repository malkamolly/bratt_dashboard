@three-rules
eyebrow: The Headline
title: Three issues. None of them kills the tree.
subtitle: But all three make the tree look terrible mid-summer.
rule: Elm Leaf Beetle | Insect. Skeletonizes leaves. Treatable with permethrin or systemic imidacloprid. Annual treatment, not a cure.
rule: Elm Leaf Miner | Different insect, similar damage pattern. Same treatment as the beetle.
rule: Anthracnose | Fungal, not insect. **Frequently confused with Dutch Elm Disease** — that's the conversation we have to manage. Two fungicide sprays in spring.


@section-divider
number: 01
title: The Two Insects
tagline: Behave like Japanese beetle: damage now, beetles dead later, leaves chewed up regardless.


@image-side
eyebrow: 1 of 3 · Elm Leaf Beetle
title: Skeletonized leaves, browning canopy.
subtitle: Cosmetic damage. Annual treatment.
body: **Non-lethal foliar damage** — the beetle chews on the leaf surface, leaving the veins behind. Tree looks rough by July.
body: **Treatment:** permethrin foliar spray or systemic imidacloprid soil drench. Behaves like Japanese beetle — **damage happens before the beetles die**, so spraying isn't a quick reversal.
body: **Set the expectation:** spraying is **annual**, not a one-and-done. It provides value up front but doesn't fix the tree long-term.
image: /topics/elm-foliar-issues/elm-leaf-beetle.jpeg
fit: cover


@image-side
eyebrow: 2 of 3 · Elm Leaf Miner
title: Tunneling damage between the leaf surfaces.
subtitle: Different insect, similar look on the tree.
body: **Larva tunnels between the upper and lower leaf surfaces**, leaving translucent or browning patches that look like small blisters or trails.
body: **Non-lethal.** Behaves similarly to the beetle in that damage occurs before the insect dies. Tree shrugs it off year-over-year.
body: **Same treatment as the beetle:** permethrin or systemic imidacloprid. Same annual cadence, same "value up front" framing.
image: /topics/elm-foliar-issues/elm-leaf-miner.jpeg
fit: cover


@section-divider
number: 02
title: The Fungal One
tagline: Anthracnose looks like DED to a panicked homeowner — and we need to manage that fast.


@image-side
eyebrow: 3 of 3 · Elm Anthracnose
title: Cupped, curling leaves with brown blotches.
subtitle: Fungal disease. Non-lethal. Frequently confused with Dutch Elm Disease.
body: **Fungal foliar disease.** Causes cupping, curling, and brown blotches on the leaves — the kind of damage that looks alarming to a homeowner who's heard of DED.
body: **It's not DED.** Anthracnose doesn't kill the tree. The first move on a homeowner-DED-panic call is to rule anthracnose in (or out) before we send anyone into full diagnostic mode.
body: **Treatment:** two fungicide sprays in the spring as leaves emerge. Annual program prevents recurrence.
image: /topics/elm-foliar-issues/anthracnose.png
fit: cover


@three-rules
eyebrow: Talking To Clients
title: How to handle the "is my elm dying?" call.
subtitle: Three sentences that calm down 90% of these conversations.
rule: It's Not DED | "What you're seeing is anthracnose (or beetle damage). It's cosmetic — your tree is not in danger. Dutch Elm Disease looks very different and we'd be able to tell you that immediately."
rule: It Comes Back Every Year | "Treatment is an annual program. We can knock the damage way down with spring treatments — but the bugs and the fungus come back, so we plan for it every year."
rule: Don't Confuse The Three | If it's chewed (skeletonized or tunneled), it's an insect. If it's cupped/curled with brown blotches, it's anthracnose. Different treatment, same talk track.
