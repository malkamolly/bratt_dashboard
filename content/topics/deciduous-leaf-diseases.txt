@three-rules
eyebrow: The Headline
title: One of these four kills trees.
subtitle: The other three look terrible but are mostly cosmetic.
rule: Cosmetic | **Apple Scab and Anthracnose** make trees look like garbage in mid-summer. They don't kill the tree. Two fungicide sprays in spring fix them.
rule: Lethal | **Fireblight** is bacterial, untreatable beyond pruning, and kills trees. Confusing it with apple scab on a crabapple is the most expensive mistake we can make.
rule: Sneaky | **Bur Oak Blight** is non-lethal on its own but weakens the tree's defenses against Two-Lined Chestnut Borer. Treat it like a slow-burning problem worth solving.


@section-divider
number: 01
title: Apple Scab
tagline: Cosmetic. Common. Easy fix.


@image-side
eyebrow: Apple Scab
title: Venturia inaequalis
subtitle: A fungal disease that makes crabapples look like Halloween in June.
body: Infects **crabapples, apple, pear, mountain ash.** Non-lethal — it damages leaves and fruit but does not kill the tree.
body: Treatable with **two fungicide sprays in the spring** about 15 days apart, while the leaves emerge. Untreated trees defoliate by mid-July.
body: **Often confused with fireblight** — but fireblight kills, scab doesn't. The distinguishing feature: scab makes brown spots; fireblight makes whole shoots wilt and stay attached.

@section-divider
number: 02
title: Anthracnose
tagline: Cosmetic. Common. Easy fix. Sound familiar?


@image-side
eyebrow: Anthracnose
title: Looks bad, isn't lethal.
subtitle: Same treatment as apple scab — two fungicide sprays in the spring.
body: Causes **cupping and curling** of leaves. Common in wet, rainy springs (as with every leaf fungal pathogen).
body: Mostly affects **white oaks (including Bur), hackberry, sugar and Norway maple, birch, walnut, elm, butternut.**
body: Frequently confused with **oak wilt** (lethal) and **Bur Oak Blight** (slow-burn). Anthracnose alone won't kill a tree; aesthetically it's less than ideal for a large front-yard specimen.

@section-divider
number: 03
title: Fireblight
tagline: The one that kills. Not treatable. Has to be cut out.


@image-side
eyebrow: Fireblight
title: Erwinia amylovora
subtitle: Bacterial. Lethal. The one we cannot afford to miss.
body: One of the most common bacterial issues in MN. Affects many **rosaceae species** — mostly apple, crab, mountain ash; also hawthorn and serviceberry.
body: **Newer shoots wilt and fall flat off the tree while still attached.** The tree looks heat-damaged. Cambial staining is similar to Dutch Elm Disease.
body: **Not treatable** beyond pruning out infected shoots. Sanitization cuts must be made **8 feet below visible staining.** Tools sanitized between cuts with **10% bleach solution.** Once it's in the trunk, the tree is done.
image: /topics/deciduous-leaf-diseases/fireblight-mountain-ash.png

@section-divider
number: 04
title: Bur Oak Blight
tagline: Slow-burn. The petioles tell the story.


@image-side
eyebrow: Bur Oak Blight
title: Tubakia iowensis
subtitle: Non-lethal alone — but it opens the door for Two-Lined Chestnut Borer.
body: Affects **Bur Oak** with wide variation in susceptibility tree-to-tree. **Petioles turn brown and stay attached to twigs all winter.** Whole leaves may stay attached too.
body: Look for **black fruiting bodies on the petioles** — that's the diagnostic. Confused with anthracnose; a tree can have both.
body: Leaves look healthy until **mid-to-late July**, then start to brown. Removing fallen leaves doesn't help — infected petioles are still on the tree.
body: **Treatment: propiconazole trunk injection in May**, before full leaf development. Treat every two years.

@three-rules
eyebrow: Talking To Clients
title: How to tell them apart in one sentence.
subtitle: Memorize these — they get you through 90% of the conversations.
rule: Apple Scab vs Fireblight | Scab makes **brown spots on leaves**. Fireblight makes **whole shoots wilt and stay attached.** If the shoot looks like a shepherd's crook, it's fireblight — get it cut out.
rule: Anthracnose vs B.O.B. | Anthracnose **cups and curls** leaves in early summer. B.O.B. shows up **mid-to-late July** with **petioles that stay on the twig all winter.**
rule: When In Doubt | A lab test (or a confident verbal diagnosis backed by a photo to a manager) is always cheaper than misdiagnosing fireblight as scab. **Err toward fireblight** when the answer matters.
